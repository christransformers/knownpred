R v Donovan [2014]

Citation: [2014] NSWChC 42 | Heard: 10–12 February 2014 | Outcome: Guilty — Juvenile Detention

Case Overview

This was a sentencing proceeding in the Children's Court of New South Wales concerning Liam Patrick Donovan (DOB: 17 December 1998), who entered a plea of guilty to one count of assault occasioning actual bodily harm contrary to s 59(1) of the Crimes Act 1900 (NSW). The offence occurred on 12 March 2013 on the grounds of Westfield High School, Baulkham Hills, New South Wales.

The victim, Liam Chen-Webber (aged 13 years at the time), was a fellow student at the school. The assault resulted in significant facial injuries including a fractured left orbital bone and the avulsion of two upper incisors. The offending was committed in the context of ongoing bullying and social conflict between the two students.

Facts of the Offence

At approximately 1:45 pm on 12 March 2013, the offender and the victim were involved in a verbal altercation in the school courtyard following a lunchtime incident in which the victim had allegedly mocked the offender about a family matter. Witnesses described the offender as appearing visibly agitated and distressed in the minutes preceding the assault.

The offender approached the victim near the eastern stairwell of the school's main building. A verbal exchange of approximately 30–45 seconds occurred, during which the victim was seated on a low wall. The offender then struck the victim twice with a closed fist to the left side of the face. The victim fell backward off the wall, striking his head on the concrete pathway. The offender then delivered two further kicks to the victim's upper torso before being pulled away by bystanders.

School staff administered first aid and emergency services attended. The victim was transported to Westmead Children's Hospital where he was treated for:

  • A displaced fracture of the left orbital rim requiring surgical intervention (orbital floor repair with implant)
  • Traumatic avulsion of teeth 11 and 21 (upper right and left central incisors)
  • Soft tissue contusions to the left cheek, temple, and right ribcage
  • Post-concussive syndrome persisting for approximately 6 weeks post-incident

The victim remained hospitalised for 4 days. He required two surgical procedures and ongoing dental rehabilitation. A victim impact statement tendered to the Court described ongoing psychological effects including anxiety, social withdrawal, and academic decline.

The force used was not a single impulsive act but a sustained and brutal attack on a smaller, vulnerable child. The kicking of a fallen victim elevates this well beyond the ordinary scope of a schoolyard fight.
— Her Honour Judge P. Morrison, Sentencing Remarks, 14 February 2014

The offender spent approximately 11 months on remand prior to sentencing, during which time he was held at the Frank Baxter Juvenile Justice Centre at Kariong. This period was taken into account by the sentencing judge pursuant to s 24 of the Crimes (Sentencing Procedure) Act 1999 (NSW).

Sentence & Orders

Her Honour Judge P. Morrison sentenced the offender on 14 February 2014. Taking into account the guilty plea (allowing a 25% discount on sentence), the offender's age and immaturity, the absence of prior criminal history, but also the objective seriousness of the offending and the significant harm caused to the victim, the Court imposed the following orders:

  • Conviction recorded for assault occasioning actual bodily harm
  • Control order of 18 months' juvenile detention, with a non-parole period of 12 months
  • Detention at Frank Baxter Juvenile Justice Centre, Kariong
  • To be transferred to a correctional facility upon attaining 18 years of age (17 December 2016) to serve the remainder of the sentence in adult custody
  • Order for the offender's DNA to be collected pursuant to s 5A of the Crimes (Forensic Procedures) Act 2000 (NSW)
You were a child when you committed this offence, but the community expects that children who cause serious harm will be held accountable. The Court's task is to balance rehabilitation with punishment and denunciation. In this case, the seriousness of the assault and the injuries sustained by the victim require a custodial sentence.
— Judge P. Morrison, Sentencing Remarks, 14 February 2014

The sentence structure reflected the legislative framework for serious children's indictable offences under the Children (Criminal Proceedings) Act 1987 (NSW). The non-parole period expired on 13 February 2015, but the offender was not released at that time due to concerns regarding ongoing behavioural issues and institutional misconduct.

Custody & Subsequent Offending

The offender's period of detention was marked by significant institutional misconduct. During his time at Frank Baxter Juvenile Justice Centre, the offender was involved in three separate physical altercations with other detainees and two incidents of property damage. These incidents resulted in loss of privileges and extensions to the initial custodial timeline.

Upon turning 18 on 17 December 2016, the offender was transferred to Lithgow Correctional Centre as a young adult offender. Within three months of transfer, the offender was charged with assaulting a correctional officer (incident date: 2 March 2017), to which he subsequently pleaded guilty in the Local Court and received an additional 6-month sentence, to be served cumulatively.

Following a successful parole application, the offender was released to parole on 13 February 2018 after serving a total of 4 years in custody (including time on remand). The parole period expired on 13 August 2018.

Key Sentencing Remarks

The following extracts from the sentencing remarks of Her Honour Judge P. Morrison were published with the judgment:

I accept that the offender has expressed genuine remorse. His early plea of guilty is consistent with that. However, remorse alone cannot undo the harm that has been done. The victim, a 13-year-old boy, has suffered serious physical injuries and will live with the psychological consequences of this assault for many years.
— At [24]
The offender's background is one of significant disadvantage. Family dysfunction, exposure to domestic violence from an early age, and an emerging diagnosis of conduct disorder explain but do not excuse his behaviour. The community is entitled to protection, but a child who has himself been a victim of violence must also be given a pathway to rehabilitation.
— At [37]
The offending occurred in a school environment, which is aggravating. Schools must be places of safety. Other students who witnessed the assault have also been affected. General deterrence, while moderated by the offender's youth, cannot be entirely disregarded.
— At [42]
